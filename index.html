Day 1 · Basics of Electronics

Network Theory & the Inside of a Charger

Dr. Anand · HOD, ECE, IIIT Kottayam

The foundation: cores of network theory, engineering electronics, and analog & digital circuits — framed by a real example, a heart-rate monitor's hardware. We opened a phone charger to trace rectification: a full-wave signal through a bridge rectifier, smoothed by RC / RLC / LC filters (RC being the most efficient), turning fluctuating DC into clean DC.

RectifiersRC FiltersVoltage DividerOhm's Law
Expand notes

Resistors & dividers

  • Resistor: two-terminal, no polarity, measured in ohms; read via colour code (incl. tolerance & multiplier).
  • Series → current same, voltage differs; add resistances. Parallel → voltage same, current differs; effective = 1/ΣR-1.
  • Potential divider: drop 12 V → 5 V with two resistors. V(r) = r / (r1 + r2).

Core laws

  • Ohm's Law: at constant temperature, V = IR.
  • KCL: total current entering a node = total current leaving.
  • KVL: total voltage rise around a closed loop = total voltage drop.
  • Ammeter → series; Voltmeter → parallel.

Exercises set

  • Online simulators / virtual labs · colour-code practice · series–parallel effective R & I · verifying Ohm's law, KCL, KVL, CDR, VDR.

Next: nodal analysis, super-mesh, super-node, superposition, and network theorems.

From Vacuum Tubes to VLSI

Dr. Vineeth · Wireless Communication

Traced the history of electronics: the Edison effect, Fleming's valve (1904), De Forest's triode (1906), the Bell Labs transistor (1947), Kilby's integrated circuit (1957), and the march through SSI → MSI → VLSI → ULSI under Moore's Law — culminating in Intel's 4004, the first 4-bit microprocessor.

HistoryTransistorMoore's LawActive/Passive
Expand notes

Components

  • Passive: resistor, capacitor (blocks DC, passes AC; stores electric field), inductor (blocks AC, passes DC; stores magnetic field). Cannot amplify.
  • Active: tube & semiconductor devices; semiconductors → transistors & diodes. Transistors amplify.

Bench equipment

  • Transformer (step-up/down), breadboard & PCB, analog multimeters, function generator, DSO, soldering iron, flux, lead.

Analog vs digital

  • Analog signals are sinusoidal with infinite values (hard to process); digital works on sampled, discrete values — preferred for IoT.
Digital Electronics · Boolean Algebra

Logic Gates, Laws & Logic Networks

Switches as transistors (X=0 / X=1) build the gates: AND = switches in series, OR = switches in parallel, NOT inverts. From there, the axioms of Boolean algebra — identity, null, idempotent, complement — and the laws that simplify real circuits.

AND/OR/NOTDe MorganSOPK-Map
Expand notes

Key laws

  • Commutative / Associative / Distributive across AND (·) and OR (+).
  • De Morgan: (A + B)' = A'·B'  and  (A · B)' = A' + B'.
  • Absorption: A + AB = A; A(A + B) = A.

Synthesis (SOP)

  • Level 1: NOT + AND gates make product terms (minterms). Level 2: a single OR gate sums them. Example: F = A'B + AB'.

Karnaugh maps

  • Gray-code grid (00, 01, 11, 10) groups adjacent 1s to drop variables; "don't care" (X) terms enable larger groups & fewer gates. Beyond ~6 variables → Quine–McCluskey / CAD.

Advantages of digital systems

  • Noise immunity · power of abstraction · modular design · faster completion · software tooling · programmability.
Combinational Logic

Adders, Subtractors, MUX/DEMUX, Encoders & Decoders

The building blocks of arithmetic and data routing: a half adder (Sum = A⊕B, Carry = AB), a full adder from two half adders, and their subtractor counterparts — then the data-movement family of multiplexers, demultiplexers, encoders and decoders.

Data movement

  • MUX — many-to-one data selector; n select lines → 2ⁿ inputs.
  • DEMUX — one-to-many distributor.
  • Encoder — 2ⁿ inputs → n outputs (e.g. 8:3).
  • Decoder — n inputs → 2ⁿ outputs (e.g. 3:8); used in memory addressing.
Sequential Logic

Latches, Flip-Flops & Counters

Where memory enters the picture — circuits whose output depends on present input and past state. From the SR latch (and its forbidden state) to the gated D latch that eliminates it, then edge-triggered and master-slave flip-flops, JK toggling, and ripple counters.

SR/D/JKMaster-SlaveRipple CounterRegisters
Expand notes

Memory elements

  • SR latch — cross-coupled NOR/NAND; S=R=1 is forbidden.
  • Gated D latch — single data input + enable; removes invalid state (D and D' always opposite).
  • Edge-triggered D FF — samples only at clock edge; Q(next) = D; stable, no race-around.
  • JK FF — fixes SR's invalid state; J=K=1 toggles. Q(next) = JQ' + K'Q.

Counters & registers

  • 3-bit & 4-bit asynchronous (ripple) up-counters; MOD-10 decade counter resets via NAND on Q3·Q1.
  • Registers (SISO, SIPO, PISO, PIPO, shift & universal) store sensor data, drive displays, handle serial/parallel conversion in IoT.
Day 5 · Microprocessors & Microcontrollers in IoT

Inside the 8085

Dr. Della Thomas · ECE Dept, IIIT Kottayam

The building blocks of IoT — sensors, processors, gateways, applications — and the data flow between them. Then deep into the 8085: its instruction cycle (fetch → decode → execute), 74 unique instructions across 246 bit patterns, assembly mnemonics, and a 5-stage pipeline for throughput.

8085Instruction SetPipelining8051 vs 8085
Expand notes

Instruction set — 5 groups

  • Data transfer (MOV, MVI, LXI, LDA/STA) · Arithmetic (ADD, SUB, INR/DCR) · Logical (ANA, ORA, CMP, rotates) · Branch (JMP, CALL, RET, conditional jumps) · Stack/IO/Control (PUSH, POP, IN/OUT, HLT, NOP).

5-stage pipeline

  • F → D → E → M → WB. Sequential: 5 instructions = 25 clock cycles. Pipelined: overlap stages so a new instruction completes nearly every cycle once filled.

Temperature-sensor scenarios

  • Scenario 1: 8085 + ADC0808 + 8255 PPI — SOC/EOC handshake transfers an 8-bit digital temperature reading.
  • Scenario 2: 8051 MCU — everything on-chip; simpler, more direct.

Tasks

  • Arrange numbers in ascending order from memory (8000H–8004H). Transmitter MCU sends 'A' continuously; receiver MCU + LCD displays it.
Data Communications & Networking

How Data Travels Well

Chapter 1 — the five components of data communication (protocol, sender, message, receiver, medium), the metrics that define quality (delivery accuracy, timeliness, jitter), transmission modes, topologies, and the standards that keep the internet interoperable.

JitterSimplex/DuplexTopologiesRFC / IEEE
Expand notes

Quality metrics

  • Delivery accuracy — right destination, uncorrupted data.
  • Timeliness — within an acceptable window (critical for voice/video).
  • Jitter — variation in packet delay; causes audio/video distortion.

Modes & structure

  • Simplex · half-duplex · full-duplex; point-to-point & multipoint.
  • Topologies: mesh, star, bus, hybrid, tree. Mesh links = n(n−1)/2.
  • Standards: IEEE 802.3 (Ethernet), 802.11 (Wi-Fi), internet RFCs.
